PHASE 01 Foundation & Recovery Week 1 → 8

The goal here is not to sprint. It's to build a stable base so everything after this compounds. You clear the backlogs, finish the ML course, start DSA from zero, and establish a daily structure. Nothing fancy. Just consistency.

⚑ Core Principle

Your brain has a finite daily budget. Don't try to do backlogs + ML + DSA all at full intensity. Sequence it: backlogs are the priority in Phase 1. Everything else runs in parallel at low intensity.

  • Download last 5 years' papers for each
  • Identify top 5 repeated topics per subject
  • Set up a fixed daily timetable (salah-anchored)
  • Continue Andrew Ng ML — minimum 1 lecture/day
  • Install Python + VS Code. Run "Hello World"
WEEK 3–4
Backlog Sprint #1

  • LeetCode: create account, solve 5 easy array problems
  • No new goals — focus and finish Subject 1
WEEK 5–6
Backlog Sprint #2
  • Subject 2 — same pattern: papers → top topics → practice
  • Andrew Ng: Gradient Descent, Logistic Regression
  • Python: NumPy basics — arrays, matrix operations
  • LeetCode: 10 more easy problems (strings + hashmaps)
  • GitHub: create account, push your first Python script
  • Write 1 LinkedIn post about what you're learning
WEEK 7–8
Backlog Sprint #3 + Wrap
  • Subject 3 — final backlog cleared
  • Andrew Ng: Neural Networks intro, forward propagation
  • Python: Pandas basics — DataFrames, CSV reading
  • LeetCode: 10 easy problems — bring total to 25+
  • Decide: AI Cyber or AI Cloud? (See Phase 2 guide below)
  • Tell Ahmed — kick off planning conversation
Daily structure (Phase 1)
  • Fajr → Study block 1 (6–8am): Backlog subject — your freshest brain goes here. 90 min max, no phone.
  • Morning → Study block 2 (10am–12pm): Andrew Ng lecture + Python exercises. Active learning, not passive watching.
  • After Dhuhr → LeetCode (30 min): Just 1–2 problems. Consistency over quantity.
  • Asr → Review + notes (30 min): What did you learn today? Write 3 bullet points. This builds retention.
  • Maghrib → Hard stop: Shut the books. Walk, train, draw, whatever. Your brain consolidates during rest, not more study.
  • Isha → Light reading only: Articles on AI, YouTube deep dives. Passive absorption. Not active work.

PHASE 02 Go Deep — Pick Your Lane Week 9 → 20

This is where you stop being a generalist and start becoming someone with a specific, rare skill combination. You finish the ML course, go deep into your chosen lane (AI Cyber or AI Cloud), build your first real project, and formalize the Ahmed partnership. Internship season is now close — everything here feeds your resume.

⚑ Choosing your lane

AI Cybersecurity = detecting threats using ML, adversarial attacks, phishing classifiers, anomaly detection in logs. More research-heavy, high growth, less competition at student level. Your maths love fits perfectly — this field is very mathematical.

AI Cloud = deploying ML models on AWS/GCP/Azure, MLOps, cost optimization, infrastructure automation. More immediately employable, lots of internship roles. More engineering-flavoured.

Decision method: In Week 9, spend 3 days building a tiny Python script in each domain. Whichever one you're still thinking about at midnight — that's your lane.

PHASE 03 Internship Sprint Week 21 → 36

This is the most intense phase. You're applying, interviewing, and continuing to build skills simultaneously. The goal is to land at least one internship — not necessarily your dream company, just something real that gets you industry experience. Your backlogs will come up. Here's how to handle them.

⚠ The backlog question

Interviewers will ask. Don't lie, don't hide it. Say: "I had a rough period personally. I've cleared 3 of my backlogs and I'm working through the rest. Here's what I've built since then." Then show the project. A real project beats a perfect CGPA story every time at the internship stage.

⚑ The compounding principle

Every project you ship, every post you write, every problem you solve — it compounds. By Month 12, you will be unrecognisable from Month 1 Faheem. Not because you did something dramatic. Because you showed up consistently when it was hard.

Content strategy (using your camera strength)
  • LinkedIn posts (3/week): "What I learned this week building [X]", "Why I chose AI + [Cyber/Cloud]", "My honest review of [resource]"
  • Video explainers (1/month): Pick one concept you just learned. Explain it on camera like you're teaching a friend. 5–8 minutes. Upload to LinkedIn + YouTube.
  • Project demos (every new release): Short screen recording + voiceover. Show what it does, show the code, show the results.
  • Ahmed handles: posting about the business side — user feedback, what problem you're solving, who it's for
  • Tone: Don't fake expertise. "I'm learning X and here's what I found" is more powerful than pretending you know everything.
